# Competitor Analysis

Three sharp competitors beat ten lazy ones. Quality of thinking > number of logos.

## Competitor categories

Always group competitors into three buckets. Missing any one is a red flag.

### Direct
Products solving the same job for the same user with a similar approach. Name 3–5.

### Indirect
Products solving the same job with a different approach — different business model, different form factor, different target persona. Name 2–3.

### Substitutes
The non-product workarounds the user does today. Usually **spreadsheets, paper, habits, or "just asking a friend."** Name the specific workaround.

Substitutes are often the *real* competitor. Most startups lose not to another startup but to "do nothing" or "keep using the spreadsheet."

## What to capture per competitor

Keep it tight — table format works well for direct competitors.

| Field | What to write |
|---|---|
| **Name + URL** | Link |
| **Target user** | Who they're really built for (not who the marketing says) |
| **Core job served** | In JTBD language |
| **Positioning / wedge** | The one-sentence pitch they lead with |
| **Pricing** | Model + entry price |
| **Traction signal** | Users, revenue, GitHub stars, funding, hiring — whichever is public |
| **Strengths** | 1–2 specific strengths |
| **Weaknesses** | 1–2 specific weaknesses (especially ones you could exploit) |

For indirect competitors and substitutes, a single paragraph each is plenty.

## Positioning map (2×2)

Pick two axes that matter in the category (e.g., price vs. depth, DIY vs. full-service, generalist vs. specialist). Plot every competitor plus your product.

The goal is to find the empty quadrant — the white space. If every quadrant has competitors, either the category is saturated or your axes are wrong (pick better axes).

Write this up as:
- The two axes and why they matter
- Where each competitor sits (with one-line reason)
- Where you sit, and why that position is defensible

ASCII version is fine:

```
         HIGH DEPTH
             |
    CompA    |    YOU
             |   (whitespace)
-------------+-------------  ENTERPRISE
   CompB     |    CompC
             |
         LOW DEPTH
```

## Feature matrix

Only useful when competitors differ in concrete capabilities that map to JTBD.

Rows = jobs or capabilities (derived from JTBD, not a feature wish-list).
Columns = competitors + you.
Cells = ✓ / partial / ✗ or a specific note.

Do not list 30 features. Pick the 5–8 that actually move purchase decisions.

## Pricing analysis

Capture three things:

1. **Model** — subscription, usage-based, one-time, freemium, marketplace take-rate
2. **Entry price and ceiling** — the cheapest and most expensive tier
3. **Hidden pricing signals** — enterprise-only tiers, annual discounts, credit-based billing

Don't just list numbers — interpret. "Competitor A starts at $29/mo but gates the core value behind the $99 tier" tells you more than a price sheet.

## Moat analysis

For each direct competitor, note *why* they're hard to unseat (if they are) and where their moat is *weakest*. Moat categories:

- **Network effects** — product gets better as more users join
- **Data moat** — proprietary data that improves the product
- **Switching cost** — user data, integrations, workflow lock-in
- **Brand / trust** — in regulated or high-stakes categories
- **Distribution** — exclusive channels, platform relationships
- **Capital / scale** — low unit costs from volume
- **Regulatory** — licenses, certifications, compliance burden

Then ask: *where does your moat form?* If you can't name one that grows with time, build one into the plan.

## White space — the payoff

Output a 2–4 bullet list titled **Whitespace and wedge thesis**. This is the punchline of the whole competitor section. Example:

> - No direct competitor targets hobbyist dog trainers; they all serve professional facilities.
> - Mobile-first experience is uniformly bad across 4 direct competitors (all are desktop-heavy CRMs).
> - None integrate with the camera roll, where trainers actually capture session notes (voice + photo).
> - **Wedge:** a mobile-first, session-note-capture tool for hobbyist trainers. Expand upward toward pros after nailing capture.

This is the output that drives the PRD. Make it sharp.
